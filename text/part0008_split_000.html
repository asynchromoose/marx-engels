To Make the World Philosophical (from Marx’s doctoral dissertation)

KARL MARX

 

Marx’s doctoral dissertation, “The Difference Between the Democritean and Epicurean Philosophies of Nature,” written between 1839 and 1841, is chiefly of interest for the following excerpts arguing that after a great world philosophy—Aristotle’s in antiquity and Hegel’s now—the system’s disciples feel an imperious urge to make the world “philosophical.” What this would mean Marx hinted in the dissertation’s foreword, where he saluted Prometheus’ revolt against the gods as a proclamation of “human self-consciousness as the highest divinity.” To transform the world in the image of Hegelian philosophy would mean to make of man in existential reality the divinity that, as Marx saw it, Hegel had already made him in thought.37

The last two paragraphs of the selection are taken from Marx’s preparatory material for the dissertation, “Notebooks on Epicurean Philosophy.”
